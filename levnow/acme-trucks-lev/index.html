Sales Letter

Dear Acme Trucks, your AI contractors are going to try to scam you.

Not because they are evil. Because they are fast, literal, and optimistic. Ask a cheap model to update a route-compliance file and it will happily cite a policy page that almost matches. Ask it to prove a maintenance chain and it may produce a clean-looking report with fake grounding.

Lev assumes that. It treats every model as a contractor who needs receipts.

“We don’t make the model smarter first. We make the task harder to fake.”

For a trucking company, that means one command like acme validate dispatch-file.json can climb a validation ladder before anyone trusts the output: lint, regex, parser, WASM, adversarial review, final boss.

The real deployment this was derived from is under NDA. The pattern is not: encode the work as constraints, run cheap gates first, send precise breadcrumbs back to weak models, and escalate only when judgment is required.

What Lev Is

A harness that teaches fast models to self-correct.

One command

The worker sees one objective: make validate pass. It never has to remember which reviewer, final boss, or semantic gate to run.

Receipts, not vibes

Every failed gate returns a breadcrumb: the field, the convention, the lookup command, and the exact policy/template file to inspect.

Cheap first

Most mistakes are caught by lint, regex, schema, parser, and WASM gates before a model token is spent.

Model escalation

When judgment is needed, FlowMind launches swappable reviewers: Composer Fast, Codex, or a stronger final-boss model.

Ralph loops

Each failure starts a fresh context with only the next few repair breadcrumbs, not the entire mess.

Constraint engineering

The system is designed so the dumb model can win by following constraints, not by being magically smart.

Acme Trucks Example

From dispatch chaos to controlled loops.

Imagine Acme Trucks has driver logs, route exceptions, maintenance work orders, and compliance letters. A weak model can draft updates quickly, but every claim has to ground to the right file, policy, inspection interval, and route event.

Lev turns that into a validation loop. If a driver-log update cites the wrong DOT rule, the validator does not just say “wrong.” It says: here is the field, here is the pattern, here is the lookup command, here is the policy page, here is the next repair.
