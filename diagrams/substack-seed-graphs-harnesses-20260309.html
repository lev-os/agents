Substack Seed

Prompts Are Converging. Harnesses Are Where Outcomes Diverge.

The most important thing we learned from graphing AI systems is not that prompts are irrelevant. It is that prompts are no longer enough. Once prompt policies begin converging, the durable differences migrate into the harness: control planes, graph/state layers, context assembly, event models, and execution boundaries. That same graph workflow may also become a hedge against statistical manipulation by making structure, provenance, and contradiction visible.

Deterministic graph workflows do not give you truth. They give you something more operationally useful: an auditable projection of reality.
1 — The Thesis

Everyone wants the leaked system prompt because it feels like the shortest path to the secret. But when you actually compile prompt corpora into comparable structures, the signal becomes obvious: the same directives, safety styles, planning behaviors, and tool-use instructions appear again and again. The differences that matter most show up in the layer around the prompt. That is why this essay is really about harnesses, not hidden strings.

Prompt Layer

Convergent

Directive language, tool etiquette, and output constraints are increasingly shared across products.

Harness Layer

Differentiating

Context, memory, eventing, graph state, and control logic shape actual system behavior.

Implication

The product is the runtime

The prompt may define the role. The harness determines what the role can reliably become.

The workflow is simple on purpose. We scan files, extract stable signals, normalize them through a shared ontology, compile them into structured artifacts, and then build two graph families: bipartite graphs for feature coverage and similarity graphs for overlap. The point is not to be fancy. The point is to make the method inspectable.

3 — Why GraphML Matters
GraphML

The portable graph object

GraphML is an XML-based graph interchange format that stores graph structure plus attributes. That makes the graph a transferable artifact, not just an in-memory calculation.

Why we use it
  • Load the same graph in Gephi or Cytoscape.
  • Keep node labels, edge weights, and metadata.
  • Archive graph versions over time.
  • Separate “graph construction” from “graph inspection.”
If the graph only exists inside the script, you have a metric. If it exists as GraphML, you have an inspectable object.
4 — Why NetworkX Matters
Graph Container

Hold the topology

NetworkX stores the graphs in ordinary Python data structures and gives us stable construction logic.

Metric Surface

Compute overlap and density

We use simple graph primitives plus Jaccard-style set overlap to build the weighted similarity graph.

Auditability

Deterministic does not mean infallible

It means inspectable. If the extractor or similarity function is wrong, the error is explicit, local, and fixable.

Primitive How we use it What it tells us
nx.Graph() Store bipartite and similarity graphs Portable, inspectable topology
write_graphml() Export graphs to GraphML Lets us separate analysis from visualization
Jaccard Compute set overlap between artifact atom sets Similarity weight between two artifacts
density() Measure how many edges exist vs possible edges How convergent or sparse a corpus is

One subtle but important lesson: deterministic workflows are not automatically correct. A wrong modeling choice can inflate or deflate a graph. The advantage is that the mistake is visible. That is the epistemic difference between an auditable workflow and a vibes-based interpretation pipeline.

Global search reasons over communities and summaries. DRIFT adds iterative refinement. Leviathan is architected differently, but the family resemblance matters: FlowMind as control plane, Graph as state/knowledge plane, Event Bus as causality spine.

7 — Graphs As A Hedge Against Manipulation
The Claim

Not truth, but a better projection

Graph workflows do not magically give you truth. They give you explicit structure, provenance, and contradiction paths that are much harder to hand-wave away.

Why this matters
  • Take ten articles on the same topic.
  • Classify them deterministically.
  • Extract entities, claims, and frames.
  • Normalize them into a shared ontology.
  • See where overlap, contradiction, and source dependence really live.

This is where the work becomes politically and epistemically interesting. In contested corpora, the graph does not need to become an oracle. It needs to become a disciplined instrument. That is exactly what the GraphRAG example over Russian and Ukrainian news illustrates: better source-grounded structure, not magical certainty. The same is true of claim-level graph verification and programmatic graph reasoning work in the literature.

The hedge is not against falsehood itself. The hedge is against untraceable blending, retrieval drift, and narrative averaging that hides origin and contradiction.
