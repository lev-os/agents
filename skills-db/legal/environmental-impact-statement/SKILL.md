# Environmental Impact Statement (EIS)

Drafts a NEPA-compliant EIS analyzing environmental consequences of a proposed federal action across all required resource categories and alternatives.

## Prerequisites

Gather before drafting:

1. **Project identification** — title, lead agency, cooperating agencies, NEPA coordinator
2. **Proposed action** — location, timing, phases, scale, legal authority, required permits
3. **Purpose and need** — underlying problem, regulatory/policy mandates driving action
4. **Alternatives** — all considered alternatives, including those screened out with reasons
5. **Baseline data** — environmental inventories (physical, biological, cultural, socioeconomic)
6. **Consultation records** — scoping comments, agency/tribal letters, Section 106/Section 7 docs
7. **Jurisdiction** — US federal; identify agency-specific NEPA implementing procedures

## Quick Start

1. Confirm project scope, lead agency, and decision to be made
2. Draft purpose and need — specific enough to bound alternatives without predetermining outcome
3. Develop alternatives array including no-action (forward-looking, per 40 C.F.R. § 1502.14)
4. Catalog affected environment by resource category
5. Analyze consequences (direct/indirect/cumulative) per resource × alternative
6. Specify enforceable mitigation measures with monitoring
7. Document all consultation and public involvement
8. Compile list of preparers with expertise and sections

## Document Structure

### 1. Project Overview

Table with: project title, lead agency + NEPA coordinator, cooperating agencies and roles, statutory authority, and specific decision to be made.

### 2. Purpose and Need

- State the problem or opportunity requiring federal action
- Cite legal/policy mandates
- Scope must bound reasonable alternatives without predetermining outcome

### 3. Alternatives Analysis

Required alternatives:

| Alternative | Requirements |
|---|---|
| No Action | Forward-looking future conditions under current management — not just present state |
| Proposed Action | Full description: location, phases, methods, ancillary facilities |
| Additional alternatives | Different location/technology/scale — must represent genuine range |
| Eliminated alternatives | Brief description + dismissal rationale (infeasibility, outside jurisdiction, fails purpose) |

### 4. Affected Environment

Organize by resource category — include only detail needed to understand conditions and trends:

- **Physical** — geology, topography, soils, minerals, paleontological resources
- **Water** — surface hydrology, groundwater, water quality, floodplains, wetlands, Wild & Scenic Rivers
- **Air** — attainment status, applicable SIP requirements
- **Biological** — vegetation, wildlife habitat, listed species, critical habitat, essential fish habitat, corridors
- **Cultural** — archaeological sites, historic structures, TCPs, NRHP properties, tribal significance areas
- **Human environment** — land use, demographics, EJ communities, recreation, visual resources, noise, transportation, treaty rights

### 5. Environmental Consequences

For each resource category × each alternative, analyze:

- **Direct** — same time and place as action
- **Indirect** — later/farther but reasonably foreseeable
- **Cumulative** — incremental contribution combined with past, present, and foreseeable future actions

Per-resource checklist:

- [ ] Intensity, duration, geographic extent
- [ ] Short-term vs. long-term; temporary vs. permanent
- [ ] Beneficial and adverse effects
- [ ] Irreversible/irretrievable resource commitments
- [ ] Significance criteria and rationale for determinations
- [ ] Scientific uncertainty or incomplete information (40 C.F.R. § 1502.22)
- [ ] Conflicts with federal/state/local plans or regulations

### 6. Cumulative Impacts

- Define geographic and temporal scope with rationale
- Identify contributing past, present, and foreseeable future actions
- Assess whether combined effects exceed resource capacity or significance thresholds
- Address resource condition trends (improving, stable, or degrading)

### 7. Mitigation Measures

Per resource category, specify for each measure:

- **Description** — specific and enforceable
- **Type** — avoid / minimize / rectify / reduce / compensate
- **Applicable alternatives**
- **Implementation** — responsible party, timing, method
- **Monitoring** — verification approach, adaptive management triggers
- **Residual impact** — what remains after mitigation and why further mitigation is infeasible

Distinguish agency-authority measures from those requiring voluntary third-party action.

### 8. Consultation and Coordination

- **Public involvement** — scoping, meetings, comment periods; how input shaped analysis
- **Draft EIS comments** — substantive comments and Final EIS responses
- **Tribal consultation** — government-to-government process, traditional knowledge, treaty rights
- **Section 106 (NHPA)** — status and findings
- **Section 7 (ESA)** — biological assessment/opinion status
- **Other coordination** — state/local agencies, other federal consultations

### 9. List of Preparers

Table with name, affiliation, expertise, and sections responsible.

## Pitfalls and Checks

- **Hard look standard** — analysis must reflect rigorous consideration; avoid conclusory statements unsupported by evidence
- **Alternatives rigging** — courts scrutinize whether alternatives represent a genuine range vs. predetermined outcome
- **No-action baseline** — must project future conditions, not merely describe current state
- **CEQ regulation versions** — 40 C.F.R. Parts 1500–1508 were revised in 2020 and 2024; confirm which version applies based on project initiation date
- **Significance factors** — address both context and intensity per 40 C.F.R. § 1508.27 (verify current section after CEQ revisions)
- **EJ analysis** — required under E.O. 12898; identify disproportionate impacts on low-income and minority communities
- **Plain language** — accessible to non-technical public; define terms; use active voice (40 C.F.R. § 1502.8)
- **Source documentation** — cite all scientific literature, regulatory standards, and monitoring data; unsupported assertions are litigation targets
- **Tables and figures** — use for spatial, quantitative, or comparative information to improve clarity
