# Schelling Fence

## Overview

**Strategic precommitment to defend arbitrary boundaries on slippery slopes**: Establish a conspicuous, defensible line that takes on significance as a coordination point, preventing gradual erosion toward undesirable outcomes.

**Core insight**: When facing slippery slopes, the strongest defense isn't always at the most justifiable position—it's at the most obvious, unambiguous line where everyone can coordinate to hold the boundary.

**Source**: Scott Alexander (Slate Star Codex), 2012, building on Thomas Schelling's concept of focal points

## The Framework

### The Slippery Slope Problem

You face a continuum from acceptable to unacceptable:
- No restrictions → reasonable restrictions → excessive restrictions → totalitarian control
- Never lie → white lies → significant lies → pathological lying
- Staying up reasonable hours → slightly late → very late → all-nighter → ruined sleep schedule

**Challenge**: Each marginal step seems small and justifiable in isolation, but the cumulative effect is disastrous.

### The Schelling Point Solution

**Schelling point**: A salient, obvious boundary that serves as a natural coordination point even without communication.

**Schelling fence**: A deliberately established Schelling point used as a precommitment device to resist incremental erosion.

**Key properties**:
1. **Conspicuous**: Easy to recognize, hard to mistake
2. **Binary**: Clear which side of line you're on
3. **Symbolic value**: Takes on significance beyond inherent properties
4. **Coordination**: Multiple parties/your future self can align on defending it
5. **Credible commitment**: Failing to hold this line means all positions up to next Schelling point are vulnerable

### Why Arbitrary Lines Work

**Not defending the optimal position**—defending the most **defensible** position:

- "No alcohol before 5pm" is more defensible than "moderate drinking only" (what's moderate?)
- "Zero tolerance for lying in engineering specs" is more defensible than "don't exaggerate too much"
- "Bedtime at 11pm always" is more defensible than "get enough sleep"

The first example in each pair is more arbitrary but clearer—that clarity makes it holdable.

## When to Use

**Personal commitment devices**:
- Habit formation (gym 5x/week, not "regularly")
- Avoiding temptation (no social media on phone, not "use less")
- Sleep/work boundaries (hard stop at 6pm, not "reasonable hours")

**Interpersonal boundaries**:
- Professional ethics (never accept gifts from vendors, not "small gifts okay")
- Relationship limits (certain topics off-limits, not "be respectful")
- Management decisions (all expenses require approval, not "use good judgment")

**Policy and governance**:
- Constitutional rights (absolute free speech, not "reasonable restrictions")
- Safety regulations (zero tolerance, not "minimize incidents")
- Institutional norms (mandatory recusal with conflicts, not "be aware of bias")

**Slippery slope prevention**:
- Whenever incremental movement toward bad outcome is easy
- When future you will face temptation to rationalize "just this once"
- When multiple parties need to coordinate on a boundary
- When erosion happens gradually and imperceptibly

## Implementation Steps

### 1. Identify the Slippery Slope

What's the undesirable end state you're trying to avoid?
- Complete sleep deprivation
- Totally compromised professional ethics
- Feature bloat that kills product simplicity
- Technical debt that makes codebase unmaintainable

Map the continuum from current state to disaster state.

### 2. Find Natural Schelling Points

Look for conspicuous boundaries along the continuum:

**Good Schelling points**:
- Round numbers (bedtime at 11pm, not 10:47pm)
- Categorical distinctions (weekdays vs. weekends)
- Bright lines (zero instances, not "very rare")
- Existing norms/traditions (industry standards)
- Simple rules (always/never, not "usually")

**Bad Schelling points**:
- Vague thresholds ("reasonable" "moderate" "appropriate")
- Subjective judgment calls (case-by-case basis)
- Complex conditions (if X and Y but not Z...)
- Invisible boundaries (hard to verify compliance)

### 3. Choose Conservative Fence Location

**Critical insight**: Place the fence **before** the truly problematic region.

**Why**: If you try to defend right at the edge of disaster, small violations are catastrophic. If you defend early, small violations are just small violations.

Example:
- **Too close**: "No drinking and driving" (violation = immediate danger)
- **Better**: "No drinking within 8 hours of driving" (violation = buffer remains)
- **Schelling fence**: "Never drink on nights before early meetings" (simple, defensive)

Build in margin for error.

### 4. Establish Symbolic Significance

Make the boundary **mean something** beyond its literal position:

**Explicit precommitment**: "I'm setting this boundary and I will defend it"

**Public commitment**: Tell others—social cost of violation reinforces boundary

**Link to values**: "This represents my commitment to X principle"

**Create ritual**: Make crossing the boundary require deliberate ceremony/justification

The boundary's power comes from its significance, not just its position.

### 5. Defend Vigorously

**Zero tolerance for violations**: Every exception weakens the fence.

**Resist "just this once"**: If it's justified this time, it'll be justified next time.

**Escalate defense**: Treat boundary violations as more serious than the marginal harm—you're defending the fence itself, not just the immediate issue.

**Make exceptions explicit**: If you genuinely need to move the fence, do it deliberately and establish a new one—don't just let it erode.

### 6. Repair Breaches Immediately

When the fence is violated:

1. **Acknowledge clearly**: Don't pretend it didn't happen
2. **Re-establish boundary**: Return to fence position, don't stay at violation point
3. **Strengthen if needed**: Add accountability or make boundary more conspicuous
4. **Learn**: What pressure led to breach? Reinforce those defenses

Every violation you tolerate becomes precedent for the next.

## Common Pitfalls

**Defending indefensible positions**: Putting the fence at an awkward, hard-to-explain place. Pick obvious boundaries even if they're not theoretically optimal.

**Too many fences**: If you have 20 bright-line rules, none are bright. Choose the critical boundaries.

**Justifying exceptions**: "This situation is special" is how fences erode. Exceptions should be extremely rare and involve moving the fence deliberately, not ignoring it.

**Arguing the fence position**: Getting into debates about whether the specific line is optimal. The fence's value is in its defense, not its specific location—don't undermine it by admitting it's arbitrary.

**Not making boundaries visible**: A fence you can't see or verify compliance with doesn't work. Make violations obvious.

**Gradual movement**: Slowly shifting the fence 5 minutes at a time. Defeats the purpose—fence should stay put or move in deliberate, discrete jumps.

## Real-World Applications

**Product development**: "No features that require >2 clicks" prevents gradual UX complexity creep. Each individual extra click seems fine, but the fence holds simplicity.

**Engineering standards**: "All code requires tests" prevents gradual erosion to "most code has tests" to "important code has tests" to "tests are optional."

**Personal productivity**: "No phone use before 9am" prevents checking "just one thing" spiral. Arbitrary but defensible.

**Free speech policy**: "No content moderation based on viewpoint" is clearer than "moderate extreme content"—the fence prevents incremental expansion of "extreme."

**Commitment devices**: "Zero alcohol during work week" easier to maintain than "moderate drinking"—no decisions, just a rule.

**Behavior change**: "Gym every weekday morning" beats "exercise regularly"—the fence is conspicuous, violations are obvious.

## Power Moves

**Pre-establish fences before temptation**: Set bedtime policy when well-rested, not when tired at midnight. Set expense rules when solvent, not when vendor offers "special deal."

**Use existing Schelling points**: Don't create arbitrary boundaries from scratch—anchor to existing round numbers, natural categories, social norms. Leverages existing salience.

**Make fence public**: Social pressure reinforces personal commitment. "I don't drink on school nights" is easier to maintain when friends know the rule.

**Celebrate fence defense**: Each time you successfully hold the boundary in face of temptation, reinforces its significance. "I wanted to stay up, but I held the 11pm line."

**Create friction at fence**: Add verification, reporting, or ceremony required to cross. Makes violations deliberate, not accidental.

**Multiple parties share fence**: Team agreement on boundary makes defense easier—"we don't ship without tests" is stronger than individual commitment.

## Advanced Technique: Fence Maintenance

**When to move a fence**:
- New information shows fence is in wrong place (too conservative or too permissive)
- Context change makes old fence irrelevant
- Successfully holding fence for long period allows moving closer to optimal

**How to move a fence without eroding it**:
1. Make movement explicit, deliberate decision
2. Establish new fence at specific new position
3. Communicate clearly: "Old rule was X, new rule is Y as of [date]"
4. Defend new fence as vigorously as old one
5. Don't move incrementally—jump to new stable position

**Never**: Just ignore the fence when inconvenient and hope no one notices. That destroys the fence's credibility.

## Related Frameworks

- **Commitment Devices**: Schelling fences are a type of commitment device using salience
- **Sorites Paradox**: The philosophical problem Schelling fences solve (heap of sand: when does removing grains stop being a heap?)
- **Precommitment Strategies**: Ulysses tying himself to mast—limiting future options
- **Trigger-Action Plans**: "When X, then Y" rules similar to fence-defense protocols
- **Bright-Line Rules**: Legal/ethical boundaries that are unambiguous
- **Chesterton's Fence**: Before removing a boundary, understand why it was erected

## Why "Fence" Metaphor Works

**Physical fence properties**:
- Visible: You know when you're crossing it
- Binary: You're on one side or the other
- Defended: Crossing requires effort/intention
- Symbolic: Represents property boundary beyond just physical barrier

**Schelling fence inherits these**: The rule isn't just a guideline—it's a defended boundary with significance beyond its specific position.
