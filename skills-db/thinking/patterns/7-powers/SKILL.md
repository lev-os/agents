# 7 Powers Strategic Framework

## Overview

7 Powers, developed by Hamilton Helmer from decades as a strategy advisor, active investor, and Stanford professor, is a rigorous framework for understanding what creates durable competitive advantage. The core insight: Power requires BOTH a benefit (improved cash flow) AND a barrier (preventing competitive arbitrage). Most strategy frameworks focus only on benefits - Helmer's breakthrough is systematically identifying which barriers actually persist. The seven powers - Scale Economics, Network Economics, Counter-positioning, Switching Costs, Branding, Cornered Resource, and Process Power - map to specific business lifecycle phases (Origination, Takeoff, Stability), giving practitioners a diagnostic tool for when each power can be created.

## When to Use

- Evaluating whether a business can achieve sustainable competitive advantage
- Deciding which strategic moves to prioritize during different growth phases
- Analyzing competitors to understand sources of their durable advantage
- Assessing investment opportunities for long-term value creation
- Diagnosing why a business is struggling despite apparent benefits
- Determining if you're competing on benefits alone (easily copied) vs. barriers
- Planning product strategy to build defensibility, not just features

## The Process

### Step 1: Understand Power = Benefit + Barrier

Identify if your advantage has BOTH components. Benefit: something that improves free cash flow (lower costs, higher prices, faster growth). Barrier: something that prevents competitors from copying or neutralizing the benefit. Without both, you have temporary advantage, not Power. **Example:** A better UI (benefit) without switching costs or network effects (barrier) = easily copied, not Power.

### Step 2: Identify Your Business Lifecycle Phase

Determine which phase you're in - Origination (pre-product/market fit), Takeoff (rapid growth post-PMF), or Stability (mature, slower growth). Different Powers can only be created in specific phases. Origination: Counter-positioning, Cornered Resource available. Takeoff: Scale Economics, Network Economics, Switching Costs available. Stability: Branding, Process Power available. **Example:** Early-stage startup (Origination) should focus on Counter-positioning or locking up a Cornered Resource, not trying to build Scale Economics yet.

### Step 3: Evaluate Scale Economics (Takeoff Phase)

Assess if your per-unit cost decreases with production volume faster than competitors can match. Requires: (1) high fixed costs and low marginal costs, (2) size advantage over competitors, (3) limited market share ceiling preventing competitors from matching scale. **Example:** Netflix content spend (fixed cost) amortized over 200M+ subscribers creates lower cost-per-subscriber than competitors with 20M subscribers.

### Step 4: Evaluate Network Economics (Takeoff Phase)

Determine if your product's value increases for each user as more users join. Requires: (1) direct network effects (users connect to users) or indirect (platform connects groups), (2) winner-take-most dynamics, (3) defensibility against multi-homing. **Example:** LinkedIn's professional network becomes more valuable as more professionals join; switching to competitor means losing access to established network.

### Step 5: Evaluate Counter-positioning (Origination Phase)

Identify if your new business model would damage incumbents' existing business if they adopted it. Requires: (1) superior economics for customers, (2) conflicting with incumbents' profit formula, (3) cognitive barrier preventing incumbents from recognizing threat. **Example:** Vanguard's index funds threatened active fund managers' high-fee model; adopting indexing would cannibalize their existing revenue.

### Step 6: Evaluate Switching Costs (Takeoff Phase)

Assess if customers face significant costs (money, time, risk, effort) to switch to competitors. Requires: (1) embedded workflows, integrations, or data, (2) retraining costs, (3) financial penalties or lost benefits. **Example:** Enterprise software with custom integrations, trained workflows, and historical data creates high switching costs; migrating to competitor takes 6-12 months and risks disruption.

### Step 7: Evaluate Branding (Stability Phase)

Determine if your brand creates pricing power or customer preference beyond rational product attributes. Requires: (1) long-term investment in brand perception, (2) emotional connection or identity association, (3) consistent delivery of brand promise over time. **Example:** Tiffany charges premium for jewelry due to brand association with luxury and romance, not superior diamonds.

### Step 8: Evaluate Cornered Resource (Origination Phase)

Identify if you have preferential access to a valuable resource competitors cannot easily obtain. Requires: (1) unique IP, talent, location, regulatory approval, or partnership, (2) barrier to competitors acquiring equivalent resource, (3) resource remaining scarce over time. **Example:** Pixar's early access to CGI talent and technology, Google's early PageRank patent, Stripe's early payments partnerships.

### Step 9: Evaluate Process Power (Stability Phase)

Assess if your embedded processes and organizational capabilities enable superior products/services competitors can't replicate. Requires: (1) complex, evolved processes refined over years, (2) tacit knowledge not easily documented, (3) organizational culture enabling process. **Example:** Toyota Production System evolved over decades; competitors can observe but struggle to replicate the culture and discipline required.

### Step 10: Choose Strategic Priorities Based on Available Powers

Based on lifecycle phase and competitive position, prioritize building Powers you can actually create now. Focus resources on moves that create both benefit AND barrier. Avoid strategies that only create benefits (features, cost cuts) without barriers - these are table stakes, not Power. **Example:** Series A startup (Takeoff phase) prioritizes building network effects or switching costs, not branding (requires years) or scale economics (need market share first).

## Example

**Netflix's Power Evolution:**
- **Origination (1997-2007):** Counter-positioning against Blockbuster - no late fees, subscription model. Blockbuster couldn't adopt without cannibalizing rental revenue.
- **Takeoff (2007-2013):** Network Economics through content recommendations and Switching Costs via personalized queues and viewing history.
- **Scale Economics (2013+):** Content spend of $17B/year amortized over 220M+ subscribers = lower cost per subscriber than competitors.
- **Cornered Resource:** Exclusive content deals and original programming (House of Cards, Stranger Things) competitors couldn't access.
- **Result:** Combined Powers created durable competitive advantage surviving Disney+, HBO Max, Amazon Prime challenges.

## Anti-Patterns

**Confusing benefits for Power:** Building better features without barriers. Competitors copy features in months; without switching costs or network effects, customers churn. **Fix:** Ask "What prevents competitors from copying this benefit?"

**Pursuing wrong Power for lifecycle phase:** Early-stage startup investing heavily in branding before achieving product/market fit. Branding requires years of consistent delivery; premature for Origination phase. **Fix:** Match Power strategy to actual business phase.

**Relying on easily-eroded barriers:** Believing first-mover advantage is durable Power. Without scale, network effects, or switching costs, being first just educates market for better-capitalized followers. **Fix:** Build real barriers (Scale, Network, Switching Costs), not timing advantage.

**Ignoring benefit side of Power equation:** Creating barriers (e.g., long contracts) without improving customer value. Customers resent lock-in without superior product. **Fix:** Power requires BOTH benefit (better product, lower cost) AND barrier.

**Treating all competitive advantages as equal:** Assuming any differentiation creates Power. Most advantages are temporary - only the seven Powers create sustainable returns. **Fix:** Rigorously test if advantage has both benefit and barrier components.

## Related Frameworks

**Good Strategy/Bad Strategy (Richard Rumelt):** Complements 7 Powers by defining strategy structure (diagnosis, guiding policy, coherent actions). 7 Powers specifies WHAT creates durable advantage; Rumelt specifies HOW to structure strategy.

**Playing to Win (Lafley & Martin):** Strategy choice cascade (where to play, how to win). 7 Powers helps answer "how to win" by identifying which Powers to build.

**Blue Ocean Strategy:** Focuses on creating uncontested market space. Counter-positioning Power explains WHY blue oceans persist - incumbents can't follow without damaging existing business.

**Crossing the Chasm (Geoffrey Moore):** Technology adoption lifecycle. Maps to 7 Powers lifecycle phases - crossing chasm = entering Takeoff phase where Scale/Network/Switching Powers become available.
