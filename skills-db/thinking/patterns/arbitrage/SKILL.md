# Arbitrage

## Overview

Arbitrage is the practice of simultaneously buying and selling an asset in different markets to profit from price discrepancies. In pure arbitrage, you earn risk-free profit by exploiting temporary inefficiencies - buying where the price is low, selling where it's high, capturing the spread. Originally a financial concept, arbitrage thinking applies broadly: geographic price differences, regulatory gaps, information asymmetries, and resource allocation mismatches all create opportunities for those who recognize them.

The key insight is that markets are imperfectly efficient. Identical goods trade at different prices due to friction (transaction costs, geography, information gaps, regulatory boundaries). Arbitrageurs perform a valuable function: by exploiting these gaps, they force prices to converge, making markets more efficient. Modern algorithmic trading has eliminated most simple financial arbitrage, but the mental model remains powerful for identifying mispriced opportunities in business, talent, knowledge, and strategy.

## When to Use

- **Market opportunities**: Identifying products trading at different prices in different regions or channels
- **Talent acquisition**: Finding undervalued expertise in overlooked markets or demographics
- **Information asymmetry**: Monetizing knowledge that isn't yet priced into markets
- **Regulatory arbitrage**: Operating where rules create favorable economics versus competitors
- **Time arbitrage**: Acting on trends or data before others recognize their significance
- **Business model innovation**: Creating value by connecting disconnected markets

## The Process

### Step 1: Identify Price Discrepancies Across Markets
Find identical or equivalent assets trading at different prices. The larger and more persistent the gap, the better the opportunity.

**Where to look**:
- **Geographic arbitrage**: Same product costs differently in different countries/cities
- **Channel arbitrage**: Product cheaper wholesale vs. retail, online vs. offline
- **Temporal arbitrage**: Predictable price patterns (seasonal, time-of-day, market cycles)
- **Information arbitrage**: Public data not yet incorporated into prices
- **Regulatory arbitrage**: Different rules in different jurisdictions create pricing gaps
- **Platform arbitrage**: Same item priced differently on Amazon, eBay, direct from manufacturer

**Example - Textbook arbitrage**: Used textbooks sell for $20 on campus bookstore buyback, $80 on Amazon. Students buy locally, sell online, capture $60 spread minus shipping/fees.

### Step 2: Verify True Equivalence
Ensure you're comparing truly identical goods. Apparent arbitrage often hides quality differences, transaction costs, or hidden risks.

**Equivalence checks**:
- Same product specification, SKU, version, condition
- Same delivery timeline and logistics requirements
- Same rights and warranties
- Same underlying asset or exposure
- No hidden costs (taxes, shipping, handling, insurance, currency conversion)

**False arbitrage warning**: "Buy BTC on exchange A for $30K, sell on exchange B for $30.5K" seems like free money. Hidden costs: transfer fees ($20), withdrawal delays (3 days, price might move), regulatory risk (frozen funds), exchange insolvency risk. Real arbitrage requires accounting for all friction.

### Step 3: Calculate Total Transaction Costs
Arbitrage only works if profit exceeds all costs. Many apparent opportunities disappear after accounting for friction.

**Cost components**:
- **Transaction fees**: Buying and selling fees, commissions, platform costs
- **Transfer costs**: Shipping, currency conversion, cross-border taxes
- **Time costs**: Your time and capital tied up during the transaction
- **Opportunity costs**: What else could you do with the capital and time?
- **Risk costs**: Probability of deal falling through, price moving against you
- **Scale limits**: Can you execute this once or repeatedly at volume?

**Profitability threshold**: Arbitrage profit must exceed total costs by enough margin to be worth the effort. Finding $5 spreads you can execute once is not a business; finding $0.50 spreads you can execute 10,000 times/day is.

### Step 4: Execute Simultaneously to Eliminate Risk
Pure arbitrage requires locking in both sides of the trade at once. Sequential trading (buy first, sell later) introduces price risk - that's speculation, not arbitrage.

**Simultaneous execution**:
- **Financial arbitrage**: Automated systems execute buy and sell within milliseconds
- **Real goods**: Secure buyer commitment before purchasing supply
- **Service arbitrage**: Lock in cost (contractor, supplier) before committing to client price

**Example - Amazon FBA arbitrage**: 
- Wrong approach: Buy 100 units at $10, list on Amazon at $20, hope they sell → Price risk
- Right approach: Confirm Amazon price stable at $20, verify competition low, calculate fees, then source units → Locked-in margin

### Step 5: Look for Structural vs. Temporary Arbitrage
Temporary arbitrage is competed away quickly. Structural arbitrage persists because barriers prevent others from exploiting it.

**Temporary arbitrage**:
- Price discrepancies due to market inefficiency
- Disappears as soon as arbitrageurs exploit it
- Example: Cryptocurrency price gaps between exchanges (now 0.1% vs. 5% in 2017)

**Structural arbitrage**:
- Based on durable advantages others can't replicate
- Geography, regulation, access, expertise, relationships
- Example: Walmart's supply chain efficiency lets them profitably arbitrage price differences between wholesale and retail that small shops can't match

**Key question**: Why hasn't someone already done this? If there's no good answer, the opportunity is temporary and competition will eliminate it.

### Step 6: Scale Through Automation or Systems
Manual arbitrage is labor-intensive and doesn't scale. Sustainable arbitrage requires systems that execute repeatedly with minimal marginal effort.

**Scaling approaches**:
- **Algorithmic trading**: Software monitors markets and executes trades automatically
- **Drop shipping**: Automated order routing from customer to supplier
- **Data scraping**: Price monitoring across thousands of sources
- **Relationship leverage**: Exclusive deals with suppliers or access to inventory
- **Process optimization**: Reducing transaction costs through efficiency

**Example - Modern crypto arbitrage bots**: Monitor order books across exchanges, execute trades within seconds when spreads exceed costs, operate 24/7 with no human intervention. Manual traders can't compete.

### Step 7: Recognize Second-Order Arbitrage Opportunities
The most valuable arbitrage exploits structural mismatches that aren't obvious price differences.

**Hidden arbitrage patterns**:
- **Talent arbitrage**: Hiring senior engineers in lower-cost markets who are as skilled as expensive SF engineers
- **Attention arbitrage**: Advertising on underpriced platforms before others discover them (TikTok ads in 2020)
- **Regulation arbitrage**: Operating in jurisdictions with favorable tax or compliance rules
- **Time zone arbitrage**: Remote workers in different time zones providing 24/7 coverage at lower cost
- **Knowledge arbitrage**: Applying expertise from one domain to solve problems in another where that knowledge is rare

**Example - Y Combinator**: Arbitrages knowledge/network (access to top VCs, startup expertise) against capital (invests $500K for 7% when startups don't realize company will be worth $100M+). Information asymmetry creates structural arbitrage.

## Example: Amazon Retail Arbitrage

**Traditional retail arbitrage**:
1. Buy discounted products at Walmart clearance ($5 each)
2. List on Amazon FBA at market rate ($20)
3. Amazon handles fulfillment, you capture $15 minus fees (~$7 after Amazon takes cut)
4. Repeat at scale with automated repricing tools

**Why it works**:
- **Geographic arbitrage**: Local Walmart inventory doesn't reflect national Amazon pricing
- **Channel arbitrage**: Retail clearance vs. online marketplace
- **Information arbitrage**: You know Amazon prices; Walmart sets clearance without checking
- **Scale arbitrage**: Individual arbitrageurs move small volumes; not worth Walmart's effort to optimize

**Limitations**:
- Labor intensive (physically shopping stores)
- Limited scalability (can't automate finding clearance)
- Inventory risk (Amazon prices can drop)
- Competition (others doing the same thing)

**Advanced version**: Retailers now use automated systems to price discriminate and close these gaps. Sustainable arbitrage requires finding structural advantages (exclusive supplier relationships, better logistics, proprietary data) rather than simple price gaps.

## Anti-Patterns

**"I'll buy now and sell when prices rise"**: That's speculation (directional bet), not arbitrage (locked-in spread).

**"The spread is huge, so profit is huge"**: Spread minus total costs equals profit. Most big spreads have big hidden costs.

**"This arbitrage opportunity will last forever"**: Pure arbitrage is competed away. Plan for opportunity to shrink or disappear.

**"I'll just manually check prices and trade"**: Human arbitrage doesn't scale and can't compete with algorithms. Need systems or structural advantages.

**"Buying low and selling high is arbitrage"**: Only if you lock in both sides simultaneously with no price risk. Otherwise it's trading.

**"I found a price gap, so I'll take out a loan to max profit"**: Leverage turns small risks into catastrophic ones. True risk-free arbitrage exists, but most "arbitrage" has hidden risks.
