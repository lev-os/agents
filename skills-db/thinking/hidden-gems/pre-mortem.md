# Pre-Mortem Analysis - Gary Klein's Prospective Risk Method

**Source**: Gary Klein - Cognitive scientist
**Year**: 2007
**Obscurity**: Tier 3 (⭐) - Moderately obscure outside PM, high in project management
**Domain**: 06-problem-solving / 13-management-strategy
**Score**: 40/50

## Origin & Context

Pre-mortem analysis developed by cognitive scientist Gary Klein in 2007 represents fundamental shift in how organizations approach risk management before projects begin. Rather than waiting for failure then conducting postmortem, Klein's method operationalizes **prospective hindsight** - mentally placing oneself in future to look backward and identify problems before they occur.

The technique has gained widespread adoption across business and management as method to increase plan success rates and combat cognitive biases undermining traditional project planning.

## Core Principle

**Imagine project has already failed, work backward to causes BEFORE starting**

Uses prospective hindsight to surface risks that planning fallacy and optimism bias hide.

## Core Principles

1. **Prospective Hindsight**: Imagining event has occurred significantly increases ability to identify reasons for that event

2. **Psychological Safety Through Framing**: Easier to voice concerns as "retrospective analysis of failed future" than direct criticism of current plan

3. **Planning Fallacy Countermeasure**: Combats systematic overconfidence about timelines, costs, resource requirements

4. **Groupthink Prevention**: Formalizes dissent as exercise's core purpose (not undermining)

5. **Preventive Not Reactive**: Improves project before investment vs learning from failure afterward

## Pre-Mortem vs Post-Mortem

### Post-Mortem (Traditional)

**Timing**: After project concludes (particularly after failure)
**Purpose**: Understand what went wrong for future learning
**Limitation**: Organization already incurred costs, wasted resources, damaged relationships/reputation
**Analogy**: Medical autopsy - everyone benefits from knowledge except the project itself

### Pre-Mortem (Proactive)

**Timing**: Beginning of project (after team briefed on plans/objectives, before significant investment)
**Purpose**: Improve project before it begins, not analyze corpse after
**Advantage**: Preventive mechanism rather than learning from failure
**Psychological**: Examines what already happened (future) vs defending against hypotheticals - engages different cognitive mechanisms, reduces defensiveness

## The Cognitive Psychology Behind Effectiveness

### Planning Fallacy and Overconfidence Bias

Teams planning projects systematically suffer from overconfidence - underestimate risks, overestimate capabilities (planning fallacy).

**Pre-Mortem Circumvents**: By asking teams to imagine failure already occurred and work backward, circumvents optimism bias clouding planning discussions.

**Reframing Effect**: "We'll never meet this deadline because we underestimated complexity" feels different when framed as retrospective analysis of failed future vs prospective criticism of current plan.

### Psychological Safety and Groupthink

Traditional planning meetings feature implicit hierarchies - junior members hesitate voicing concerns about senior leaders' proposals.

**Pre-Mortem Breaks Dynamic**: Formalizing dissent as exercise's core purpose. Everyone plays hypothetical scenario vs directly criticizing proposal → knowledgeable dissenters feel safer speaking up.

### Prospective Hindsight Enhancement

Research shows imagining event has already occurred significantly increases ability to identify reasons. This phenomenon (formalized by Klein from earlier work) explains why teams generate more realistic threat assessments when imagining future failure than asking "what might go wrong."

## 5-Step Pre-Mortem Process

### Step 1: Preparation and Timing (Before Session)
**What**: Conduct immediately after initial team briefing
**When**: After goals/plans introduced but before deep investment
**Why**: Team understands project sufficiently to generate meaningful threats, but not so invested they defensively resist criticism
**Duration**: 45 minutes to 1 hour (comprehensive), 20-30 minutes (abbreviated)

### Step 2: Mental Transportation into Future (5 min)
**What**: Use dramatic language to mentally transport team into future where project failed
**How**: "Close your eyes. Imagine it's 6 months from now. Project is complete. Unfortunately, it's been complete failure - utter disaster. Unexpected events derailed us at every turn. The project died. Keeping yourself in that future failure, slowly open eyes."
**Output**: Team emotionally/imaginatively engaged in failed future state
**Example**: Include timeline markers making future feel real (actual events expected during project period)

### Step 3: Systematic Threat Generation (15-20 min)
**What**: Generate plausible reasons for project failure
**How**: Each team member contributes, responses recorded (Post-Its on visible surface)
**Output**: Comprehensive threat inventory (15-30 threats typically)
**Example**: Divergent generation (breadth > immediate judgment), all suggestions accepted without evaluation
**Key**: Leaders/seniors resist urge to dismiss or reassure during this phase

### Step 4: Threat Analysis and Prioritization (15-20 min)
**What**: Examine each threat for magnitude and likelihood
**How**: Evaluate which could realistically derail, which are interconnected, which most actionable
**Output**: Prioritized threat list with root causes identified
**Example**: Several surface threats may stem from common root cause - address systemic vulnerability vs symptoms

### Step 5: Preventive Action Planning (10-15 min)
**What**: Develop preventive actions or mitigation strategies for significant threats
**How**: For each major threat, create specific actionable response
**Output**: Documented threat lists + corresponding mitigations integrated into project plan
**Example**: Additional contingency time, clearer requirement specs, early pilot testing, stakeholder comms, resource buffers

## What Pre-Mortems Surface

### Systemic and Organizational Risks

Often remain hidden in traditional planning because they reflect organizational weaknesses not project-specific issues.

**Examples**:
- "Our approval processes too slow and decisions made by people who don't understand technical constraints"
- "We consistently over-commit team members across multiple projects"

These reflect organizational patterns determining project success/failure.

### Interdependency Risks

Complex projects depend on external vendors, regulatory approvals, parallel initiatives. Traditional planning treats these as fixed constraints; pre-mortems help think through how delays/changes cascade through project timeline.

### Early Warning Indicators

Beyond identifying threats, pre-mortems sensitize team members to early signs of trouble. When team has imagined specific failure scenarios, they notice early indicators more quickly during execution → faster adaptive responses.

### Stakeholder Misalignment

Pre-mortems with cross-functional stakeholders often reveal different groups have fundamentally different success criteria or risk tolerances. Surfacing misalignments early allows alignment conversations before they become hidden resentments.

## Integration

**Complements**:
- Project risk registers (outputs flow directly)
- Agile retrospectives (similar psychological framing)
- Murphyjitsu (more adversarial variant)
- Scenario planning (explores alternatives)

**Conflicts with**:
- Pure optimism/positive thinking cultures
- Blame-oriented environments (threatens psychological safety)
- Time-pressured launches (can't spend time on analysis)

**Leads to**:
- Improved project planning
- Realistic risk assessment
- Stakeholder-aligned expectations
