# Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH)

**Source**: Richards Heuer Jr. - CIA intelligence analysis
**Year**: 1978-1986 development, 1999 publication
**Obscurity**: Tier 2 (⭐⭐) - High (intelligence → spreading to business)
**Domain**: 04-decision-making / 06-problem-solving
**Score**: 42/50

## Origin & Context

The Analysis of Competing Hypotheses represents one of the most influential structured analytical techniques in intelligence analysis, developed by Richards Heuer Jr. between 1978-1986 while serving as an analyst at the Central Intelligence Agency.

Heuer synthesized insights from scientific method, cognitive psychology, and decision analysis to create a methodology specifically designed to combat cognitive biases plaguing intelligence professionals. The technique gained wider recognition following the 1999 publication of "Psychology of Intelligence Analysis," which became the foundational text for teaching analysts to overcome systematic reasoning flaws.

## Core Principle

**Actively search for evidence to DISconfirm alternative hypotheses, not to confirm favored ones**

This inverted reasoning approach directly counters confirmation bias - the natural tendency to seek, interpret, and remember information consistent with existing beliefs.

## Core Principles

1. **Mandatory Alternative Consideration**: All plausible explanations must receive equal initial consideration

2. **Disconfirmation Focus**: Ask "What would prove this FALSE?" not "What supports this?"

3. **Transparent Reasoning**: Matrix makes logic explicit, auditable, reviewable

4. **Diagnostic Evidence Prioritization**: Evidence distinguishing between hypotheses > evidence confirming any single one

5. **Systematic Assumption Challenge**: Explicitly re-examine foundational premises

## The ACH Matrix Structure

### Matrix Architecture

**Columns**: Hypotheses (mutually exclusive explanations)
**Rows**: Evidence (facts, arguments, assumptions, deductions)
**Cells**: Consistency markers (+ or −)

- **+ (Consistent)**: Evidence would likely be observed if hypothesis true
- **− (Inconsistent)**: Evidence unlikely/impossible if hypothesis true
- **± (Ambiguous)**: Partial fit, needs clarification

### Matrix Functions

1. **Transparency**: Makes reasoning auditable by decision-makers
2. **Comprehensiveness**: Forces consideration of ALL relevant evidence
3. **Diagnostic Identification**: Reveals which evidence best distinguishes hypotheses

## The 8-Step ACH Process

### Step 1: Hypothesis Generation
**What**: Divergent thinking to generate all plausible explanations
**How**: Brainstorm without constraints, then eliminate redundant/irrational ones
**Output**: Mutually exclusive, collectively exhaustive hypothesis set
**Example**: Military asset relocation → (A) Offensive prep, (B) Defensive response, (C) Training, (D) Logistics, (E) Doctrine change

### Step 2: Evidence Compilation
**What**: Comprehensive information gathering
**How**: Include facts, deductions, assumptions, goals, procedures - interpret broadly
**Output**: Exhaustive evidence list (15-25 items typically)
**Example**: Satellite imagery timing, intercepted comms, leadership transitions, historical patterns, economic indicators

### Step 3: Matrix Construction
**What**: Systematically evaluate each hypothesis-evidence pair
**How**: Mark consistency (+/−/±) for every cell in matrix
**Output**: Complete matrix with explicit judgments
**Example**: "Rapid timing" → + for offensive/defensive, − for training/logistics

### Step 4: Matrix Refinement
**What**: Reassess hypotheses and evidence based on complete picture
**How**: Combine overlapping hypotheses, consolidate redundant evidence, remove unsupported alternatives
**Output**: Refined matrix
**Example**: Split "Offensive Prep" into "Large-scale" vs "Limited strike" (different evidentiary requirements)

### Step 5: Tentative Conclusions
**What**: Analyze matrix to identify relative likelihood
**How**: Prioritize by DISCONFIRMING evidence (not confirming)
**Output**: Probability ranking of hypotheses
**Example**: Hypothesis with strong disconfirming evidence downranked despite some confirming evidence

### Step 6: Critical Evidence Reevaluation
**What**: Re-examine key assumptions and strongest disconfirming evidence
**How**: Question logic, test assumptions, consider alternative interpretations
**Output**: Validated or revised conclusions
**Example**: Verify intercepted comms actually refer to military relocation, not different context

### Step 7: Conclusions and Reporting
**What**: Communicate findings with full reasoning transparency
**How**: Report leading hypothesis, key evidence, alternatives considered, disconfirming evidence
**Output**: Transparent analytical report with confidence levels
**Example**: "Most likely: logistical consolidation (65% confidence), defensive response remains alternative (25%)"

### Step 8: Future Observation Milestones
**What**: Specify what future observations would shift probabilities
**How**: Define specific, observable indicators for each hypothesis
**Output**: Monitoring framework for updating analysis
**Example**: "If tactical air support deploys to forward bases → increase offensive prep probability"

## Why ACH Prevents Confirmation Bias Better

### Three Primary Mechanisms

**1. Mandatory Alternatives**: Requiring multiple competing hypotheses prevents premature closure on single explanation

**2. Disconfirmation Focus**: "What would prove this FALSE?" leverages principle that disconfirming evidence is more diagnostic than confirming

**3. Explicit Assumption Challenging**: Step 6 specifically forces re-examination of foundational premises (many analytical failures trace to unchallenged assumptions)

### Comparative Advantage

Unlike intuitive judgment or simple evidence weighing, ACH creates explicit, reviewable reasoning record. Alternative methods leave logic implicit, allowing cognitive biases to operate undetected. Matrix structure makes all reasoning visible and challengeable.

## Real-World Applications

### Intelligence Analysis (Original Domain)
- Assessing adversary intentions
- Evaluating emerging threats
- Interpreting ambiguous signals
- Interagency consensus building on ambiguous situations

### Business Competitive Intelligence
**Example**: Competitor reduces prices in key segment

**Hypotheses**:
- (A) Pursuing market share dominance
- (B) Facing financial difficulties
- (C) Responding to excess inventory
- (D) Testing price sensitivity

**Evidence**: Financial health, capacity utilization, positioning statements, historical patterns
**ACH Application**: Recent capital expenditures inconsistent with financial difficulty (B), consistent with market dominance (A)

### Criminal/Legal Investigation
- Multiple suspect scenarios
- Accident analysis with competing explanations
- Physical evidence evaluation against alternative theories
