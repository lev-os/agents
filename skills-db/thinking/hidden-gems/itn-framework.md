# ITN Framework (Importance, Tractability, Neglectedness)

**Source**: Effective Altruism (80,000 Hours, Holden Karnofsky/GiveWell 2013)
**Year**: 2013-2014 development
**Obscurity**: Tier 2 (⭐⭐) - High (EA community → spreading)
**Domain**: 04-decision-making / 13-management-strategy
**Score**: 41/50

## Origin & Context

The ITN framework was developed by Holden Karnofsky around 2013 for GiveWell Labs. 80,000 Hours presented a quantitative version developed by Owen Cotton-Barratt in late 2014.

The framework operates as a mathematical equation to identify which problems deserve additional resources by quantifying expected impact of marginal effort.

## Core Principle

**Score causes on 3 dimensions to find neglected high-leverage opportunities**

Formula: Expected Impact = Importance × Tractability × (1/Neglectedness)

Or: MU/$ = Importance × Tractability

## Core Principles

1. **Marginal Focus**: Evaluate marginal impact (one more unit of effort), not total cost-benefit

2. **Multiplicative Structure**: Three dimensions multiply (if any approaches zero, impact collapses)

3. **Neglectedness as Multiplier**: High neglectedness = left side of diminishing returns curve = higher marginal impact

4. **Cause-Impartial Comparison**: Enables principled comparison across different domains

5. **Expected Value Maximization**: Consequentialist framework for resource allocation

## The Three Dimensions

### Importance (Scale)
**Definition**: Overall magnitude/badness of problem if left unsolved
**Components**:
- How many beings affected?
- How severely are they affected?
**Metrics**: DALYs, lives saved, income improvements, suffering prevented
**Example**: Malaria importance = millions affected × severity of illness

### Tractability (Solvability)
**Definition**: How effectively additional resources solve/reduce the problem
**Question**: What fraction of problem solved per dollar/person?
**Key**: Depends on current resources (diminishing returns)
**Example**: First dollar prevents 10 cases, millionth dollar prevents 0.1 cases

### Neglectedness
**Definition**: Total current resources and attention problem receives
**Inverse Indicator**: More neglected = more room for marginal impact
**Why**: Operates further left on diminishing returns curve
**Example**: Nuclear risk receives 1/100th of climate funding despite comparable importance

## Mathematical Foundation

Why multiplication matters:

**If any dimension → 0, total impact → 0**:
- Important but intractable = minimal marginal value
- Tractable but unimportant = low justification
- Important + tractable but saturated = diminishing returns

**Unit cancellation**: Properly formulated, units cancel to yield "good done per extra dollar"

**Neglectedness amplifies**: Same importance + tractability → higher impact if more neglected

## Step-by-Step ITN Analysis

### Step 1: Problem Definition (15 min)
**What**: Clearly articulate specific problem (not broad cause)
**How**: Be precise - "river blindness in West Africa" not "global health"
**Output**: Measurable problem definition
**Example**: "Vaccine-preventable diseases in sub-Saharan Africa"

### Step 2: Quantify Importance (30 min)
**What**: Gather evidence about scale and severity
**How**: Research affected populations, select welfare metrics (DALYs, etc.), cross-reference sources
**Output**: Importance estimate (relative or absolute)
**Example**: 500,000 deaths/year, 5M DALYs, affects 50M people

### Step 3: Assess Tractability (45 min)
**What**: Investigate evidence that additional effort improves outcomes
**How**: Document existing interventions + effectiveness, analyze scalability, assess obstacles
**Output**: Tractability estimate
**Example**: "10% reduction possible with 2x funding" or "proven interventions exist and scale"

### Step 4: Establish Neglectedness (30 min)
**What**: Quantify current resource allocation
**How**: Compile annual funding (philanthropic + government), research talent allocation, assess institutional capacity
**Output**: Neglectedness baseline
**Example**: "$50M/year philanthropic, $200M government vs $10B for similar-importance problem"

### Step 5: Synthesize and Score (20 min)
**What**: Comparative reasoning across dimensions
**How**: Rate as strong/moderate/weak, consider interactions
**Output**: Qualitative ITN judgment
**Example**: High importance + high tractability + very high neglectedness = compelling opportunity

### Step 6: Compare Against Alternatives (30 min)
**What**: Evaluate multiple candidate causes using same framework
**How**: Parallel ITN analysis, identify strongest combination
**Output**: Prioritization ranking
**Example**: Nuclear security vs malaria vs animal welfare - which has best ITN profile?

## Real Examples from Effective Altruism

### Climate Change
- Importance: Extremely high (billions affected, civilizational threat)
- Tractability: High (clear policy solutions, societal mobilization exists)
- Neglectedness: Moderate-to-low (substantial governmental + philanthropic attention)
- **ITN Conclusion**: Despite importance, marginal funding may offer lower impact than neglected alternatives

### Nuclear War Risk
- Importance: Potentially very high (civilizational threat)
- Tractability: High (proliferation controls, diplomacy, technical safety show promise)
- Neglectedness: Very high (~1/100th of climate funding)
- **ITN Conclusion**: Compelling marginal impact case

### Malaria Prevention
- Importance: Extremely high (hundreds of millions affected)
- Tractability: High (bed nets, antimalarials proven)
- Neglectedness: Was high historically, declining as funding increased
- **ITN Evolution**: As neglectedness declined, marginal resources might flow elsewhere

### Animal Welfare Advocacy
- Importance: Extremely high (billions of animals in industrial agriculture)
- Tractability: Moderate (behavior change difficult but possible)
- Neglectedness: Very high (minimal philanthropic attention relative to importance)
- **ITN Conclusion**: High scores among EA focused on moral expansion

## Difference from Traditional Cost-Benefit

| Aspect | ITN Framework | Traditional Cost-Benefit |
|--------|--------------|------------------------|
| **Focus** | Marginal impact | Total project impact |
| **Structure** | 3 multiplicative dimensions | Single metric ($/benefit) |
| **Allocation** | Explicit diminishing returns | Secondary adjustment |
| **Comparison** | Cross-sector principled | Within-sector typical |

## Why Hidden Gem

**Why Obscure**:
- Effective Altruism community origin (niche movement)
- Recent development (2013-2014)
- Requires consequentialist worldview (not universal)
- Quantitative approach seems cold/utilitarian
- Not popularized in mainstream philanthropy

**Why Powerful**:
- Systematic framework for maximizing impact
- Prevents resource waste on low-leverage causes
- Identifies neglected opportunities systematically
- Enables principled cross-domain comparison
- Documented impact improvements (EA applications)

**Cross-Domain**:
- Philanthropic giving (cause selection)
- Career choices (where to work)
- Research prioritization (what to study)
- Policy analysis (where to invest public resources)
- Business strategy (market selection)
