# Jobs to Be Done (JTBD) - Customer Motivation Framework

**Source**: Clayton Christensen (influenced by practitioner blogs, 1990s-2010s)
**Year**: Developed through "Competing Against Luck" and adoption in startup/product circles
**Obscurity**: Tier 2 (⭐⭐) - High outside product management, spreading
**Domain**: 14-product-development / 11-ui-ux
**Score**: 39/50

## Origin & Context

The Jobs to Be Done framework emerged from Clayton Christensen's work (Harvard Business School professor known for disruption theory) combined with practitioner adoption in startup and product circles. Developers included Bob Moesta and Tony Ulwick (innovation strategist, formerly IBM).

The fundamental insight: customers don't buy products; they "hire" them to accomplish specific goals. This conceptual shift reframes purchasing decisions from feature-based to problem-solving based.

## Core Principle

**Customers "hire" products for jobs, not for features**

A job to be done is "a circumstances-based description of understanding customers' desires, competitive set, anxieties, habits, and timeline of purchase."

Instead of focusing on customer demographics (age, gender, income), JTBD examines WHY customers make buying decisions by understanding underlying circumstances and motivations.

## Core Principles

1. **Circumstance Over Demographics**: Purchase decisions driven by situations, not customer categories

2. **Three-Dimensional Jobs**: Every job has functional, emotional, and social dimensions

3. **Hire Metaphor**: Customers actively select solutions to accomplish jobs (not passive purchases)

4. **Progress-Seeking**: Customers want to make progress in their lives (the "job" is enabling that progress)

5. **Competitive Set Redefinition**: Competition includes all alternatives customers might "hire" for the job (often non-obvious)

## Three Dimensions of Jobs

Clayton Christensen: "With few exceptions, every job people need or want to do has a social, a functional, and an emotional dimension."

### Functional Jobs

**Definition**: Practical, tangible outcome customers seek

**Characteristics**:
- Explicit task completion
- Measurable performance
- Often consciously articulated

**Examples**:
- Get from point A to B (transportation)
- Share information with team (collaboration)
- Track project progress (project management)
- Make hole in wall (drill purchase)

### Emotional Jobs

**Definition**: How customers want to feel or what they want to avoid feeling

**Characteristics**:
- Psychological and self-image components
- Often operate beneath conscious awareness
- Drive purchasing more powerfully than functional alone

**Examples**:
- Feel confident in decisions (business software)
- Avoid embarrassment (personal care products)
- Gain recognition (luxury goods)
- Feel "like themselves again" (Snickers example)

### Social Jobs

**Definition**: How customers want to be perceived by others

**Characteristics**:
- Status signaling
- Group belonging
- Identity expression

**Examples**:
- Look professional (business attire)
- Be seen as innovative (early tech adoption)
- Maintain reputation (quality tools for professionals)
- Signal values (sustainable/ethical products)

## Job Story Format vs User Stories

### User Stories (Traditional Agile)

**Format**: "As a [user type], I want to [action], so that [benefit]"

**Focus**: User characteristics and desired features

**Example**: "As a busy parent, I want to quickly find recipes, so that I can cook dinner faster"

**Problem**: Centers on user identity, risks demographic assumptions

### Job Stories (JTBD)

**Format**: "When [situation], I want to [motivation], so I can [expected outcome]"

**Focus**: Triggering circumstances and underlying motivations

**Example**: "When I'm rushing home from work and need dinner ideas, I want to quickly identify meals I can make with ingredients on hand, so I can feed my family without additional shopping trips"

**Advantage**: Strips away demographics, forces investigation into actual circumstances prompting behavior

## The Classic Milkshake Study

### Traditional Product Development Approach

Fast food company wanted to improve milkshakes. Traditional approach: survey buyers about taste preferences, texture, flavor varieties.

### JTBD Research Approach

Instead of asking "what features do you want," researchers observed WHEN people bought milkshakes and WHY.

### Discovery

**Timing**: Many purchases occurred during morning commutes

**Functional Job**: Provide substance to sustain through long morning drives
- Something that lasts entire commute
- Easy to consume one-handed while driving
- Fills stomach more than coffee

**Emotional Job**: Feel satisfied and energized for morning
- Ready to tackle work day
- Comfortable during commute

**Insight**: Milkshake improvements should focus on:
- Convenience (one-handed consumption)
- Sustained satiation (lasts full commute)
- Flavor durability (doesn't get boring)
- NOT on taste complexity or richness conventional product development might have pursued

### Competitive Set Reframed

**Traditional View**: Milkshake competes with other milkshakes

**JTBD View**: Milkshake competes with:
- Bagels (but crumbly while driving)
- Bananas (gone in 3 bites, too quick)
- Coffee (doesn't fill stomach)
- Snickers bar (melts on dashboard)

Understanding true competitive set reveals which aspects of the job are underserved.

## JTBD Research Interview Process

### Key Questions

**Trigger Question**: "Tell me about the last time you [purchased/used product]"
- Gets specific instance, not generalized opinion

**Motivation Question**: "What were you trying to accomplish?"
- Uncovers underlying job, not just stated reason

**Current Solution**: "How do you handle this today?"
- Identifies what customer currently "hires" for job

**Pain Points**: "What's frustrating about current approach?"
- Reveals underserved aspects of job

**Ideal Solution**: "In a perfect world, how would this work?"
- Surfaces aspirational job completion

### Analysis Framework

**1. Identify Job Executor**: Who is trying to get job done?
- Not demographics but role in specific circumstance

**2. Define Job Context**: When/where does this job arise?
- Triggering situations creating job need

**3. Uncover Motivation**: What progress are they seeking?
- Deeper "why" beyond surface task

**4. Understand Obstacles**: What prevents job completion?
- Barriers, anxieties, switching costs

**5. Measure Success**: How do they define success?
- What does "job well done" look like?

## Opportunity Prioritization

**Opportunity Score** = (Importance + Satisfaction Gap) × Frequency

### Importance
How critical is job completion to customer?
- Essential daily activity = high
- Nice-to-have occasional task = low

### Satisfaction Gap
How well do current solutions work?
- (Importance rating) - (Current satisfaction with solutions) = Gap
- Larger gap = bigger opportunity

### Frequency
How often does this job occur?
- Daily = high frequency multiplier
- Yearly = low frequency multiplier

**Result**: Highest opportunity scores = most important, poorly-served, frequent jobs

## Application to Product Development

### Organize Around Jobs Not Demographics

**Traditional**: Organize by user segments (millennials, enterprise, SMB)
**JTBD**: Organize around jobs being done

**Implications**:
- R&D, design, marketing, sales integrate around targeted job
- Prioritize features contributing to job completion
- Differentiate by understanding underserved job aspects
- Avoid disruption by excelling at specific jobs

### Product Roadmap Decisions

**Question Shift**:
- From: "What features should we add?"
- To: "What experiences deliver perfect job completion?"

**Prioritization**:
- Features enabling core job = high priority
- Features unrelated to job = low priority (even if "cool")
- Reduce scope creep by asking "does this serve the job?"

## Snickers Example: "You're Not You When You're Hungry"

**Campaign Insight**: Identifies WHY people "hire" Snickers

**Jobs**:
- **Functional**: Hunger satisfaction + energy restoration
- **Emotional**: Feeling like yourself again (not irritable/weak from hunger)
- **Social**: Avoiding embarrassment/friction of being hangry

**Not About**:
- Chocolate content
- Caloric value
- Nutritional information

**Success**: Speaks directly to circumstance (hungry, need quick fix) and outcome (feel normal again)

## Real Applications

### P&G Case (from earlier)

Pampers Dry Max - extensive parent/baby research revealed job wasn't just "keep baby dry" but specifically:
- **Functional**: Thin for comfort + absorbent for performance (traditional approaches accepted trade-off)
- **Emotional**: Parents feel competent/caring
- **Social**: Good parenting signal

Result: Product simultaneously thinner AND more absorbent (contradicting assumed constraints).

## Step-by-Step JTBD Implementation

### Step 1: Define Core Job (Research Phase - 2-4 weeks)
**What**: Identify primary job through customer interviews focused on circumstances
**How**: Ask about last purchase, motivations, current solutions, frustrations
**Output**: Job statement in circumstances-based language
**Example**: "When I'm commuting and need sustained energy, I want convenient filling options, so I can stay alert through my drive"

### Step 2: Map Three Dimensions (Analysis - 1 week)
**What**: Articulate functional, emotional, social aspects
**How**: Extract from interviews which aspects matter most
**Output**: Three-dimensional job description
**Example**: Functional = sustaining energy; Emotional = feel prepared for day; Social = efficient professional image

### Step 3: Identify Contextual Factors (Analysis - 1 week)
**What**: Map circumstances influencing job execution
**How**: When does job arise? What constraints exist? What emotional states present?
**Output**: Contextual job landscape
**Example**: Morning timing, one-handed consumption need, price sensitivity, time pressure

### Step 4: Analyze Competitive Alternatives (Research - 1-2 weeks)
**What**: Understand what customers currently "hire" for this job
**How**: Ask "what else have you tried?" and "what do you use instead?"
**Output**: True competitive set (often non-obvious)
**Example**: Not just other milkshakes but bagels, bananas, coffee, energy bars

### Step 5: Design Experiences Around Job (Design - Ongoing)
**What**: Create product experiences supporting perfect job completion
**How**: Every touchpoint optimized for job execution in identified circumstances
**Output**: Job-centered product design
**Example**: Milkshake thick enough to last commute, easy one-handed cup, available early morning

### Step 6: Organize Teams and Priorities (Organizational - Ongoing)
**What**: Align organization around delivering core job
**How**: Cross-functional integration around job (not functional silos)
**Output**: Job-focused organization
**Example**: "Morning commute sustenance team" integrating R&D, design, marketing, sales

### Step 7: Measure Job Completion Success (Metrics - Ongoing)
**What**: Track how well product enables job completion
**How**: Metrics based on job outcomes, not feature adoption
**Output**: Job performance dashboard
**Example**: "% of customers who feel sustained through commute" not "milkshake sales volume"
