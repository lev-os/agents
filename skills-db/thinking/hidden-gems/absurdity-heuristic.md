# Absurdity Heuristic - Rationality Cognitive Bias

**Source**: LessWrong rationality research
**Year**: 2000s rationality movement
**Obscurity**: Tier 3 (⭐) - Moderately obscure (rationalist community)
**Domain**: 04-decision-making / 02-cognitive-biases
**Score**: 38/50

## Origin & Context

The absurdity heuristic is a mental shortcut that causes us to dismiss highly atypical or unprecedented situations as impossible or implausible. Rather than rigorously evaluating whether something could occur, we classify it as "absurd" based on how far it deviates from our typical experience.

While this mechanism often serves as useful epistemic hygiene helping filter genuine nonsense, it frequently causes us to underestimate or completely reject possibilities that are statistically rare but very real.

The concept emerged from rationality research exploring how humans make decisions under uncertainty, building on Herbert Simon's work establishing that humans rely on mental shortcuts rather than exhaustive rational analysis.

## Core Principle

**We dismiss highly atypical events as impossible based on "common sense" - overweighting limited experience**

A type of representativeness heuristic where intuitive judgment about whether something is "normal" becomes basis for confidence in its possibility.

## Core Principles

1. **Past as Template**: We use past experiences and current mental models as template for expectations

2. **Deviation Equals Impossible**: When reality deviates significantly from template, we label it impossible (not just surprising)

3. **Confusion of Unlikely with Impossible**: We treat low probability as impossibility (dangerous for high-impact tail risks)

4. **Limited Sample Bias**: Each person experiences ~30,000 days of life - infinitesimal sample of what can happen

5. **Common Sense as Trap**: Intuition based on severely limited sample, yet we trust it absolutely

## The Mechanism: Overweighting Common Sense

### How Common Sense Becomes Trap

Common sense emerges from patterns we've observed repeatedly. When something violates these dramatically, our intuition screams "impossible!" or "absurd!"

Yet this intuition is based on severely limited sample:
- We each experience perhaps 30,000 days of life
- Black swan events fall outside typical experience by definition
- Media consumption reinforces common patterns, underreports rare events

**The Error**: Absurdity heuristic leads us to require extraordinarily high evidence thresholds for atypical claims, even when base rate considerations suggest they're quite possible.

### Distinguishing "Unlikely" from "Impossible"

Critical error the heuristic enables:

**Something can be**:
- Extremely unlikely (1 in a million)
- Entirely possible (consistent with all known physics/economics)
- Still classified as "absurd" by our intuition

This confusion becomes catastrophic when dealing with tail-risk events having massive consequences if they occur.

## Connection to Black Swan Thinking

The absurdity heuristic is essentially the ENEMY of Black Swan awareness.

**Black Swans** (Nassim Taleb):
1. Outside realm of regular expectations
2. Carrying extreme impact
3. Rationalized only after the fact

**Absurdity Heuristic Explains**: Why Black Swans are so commonly dismissed before they occur

- Pandemic killing millions seemed absurdly unlikely in 2019
- Financial instruments creating systemic risk seemed absurdly complex yet manageable before 2008
- Cyberweapons shutting down infrastructure seemed science fiction before Stuxnet

**Bidirectional Problem**:
- (a) Causes us to underestimate probability of rare events
- (b) Prevents us from taking seriously warnings of those who didn't find event absurd

## When the Heuristic Helps vs When It Blinds

### When It Helps (Genuine Value)

**Protection against pseudoscience**: Dismissing perpetual motion machines violating known physics is appropriate

**Resource allocation**: Can't investigate every wild claim with equal rigor; preliminary dismissal necessary

**Decision-making speed**: Time-constrained situations - heuristic enables quick judgments often correct

**Social coordination**: Shared standards for "absurd" help communities maintain coherent discourse

### When It Blinds (Dangerous)

Heuristic becomes dangerous precisely when:

**Low-probability, high-impact**: 0.1% annual probability of 50% market crash sounds "absurd" but deserves serious risk management

**Limited reference class**: Past stability doesn't prove future safety, yet heuristic treats it as near-certain evidence

**Expert consensus wrong**: Expertise provides better calibration than intuition, but heuristic applies to both

**Domain-specific knowledge contradicts intuition**: Complex systems produce counterintuitive outcomes; common sense particularly unreliable in domains with:
- Nonlinear dynamics
- Emergent properties
- Multi-agent interactions

**Blinds most severely in**: Finance, epidemiology, climate systems, technology adoption, geopolitics

## Historical Examples of Heuristic Failures

### COVID-19 Pandemic (2019-2020)

**Dismissals**:
- Pathogen shutting down global economies seemed absurd to most policymakers
- Epidemiologists' warnings dismissed as absurdly pessimistic/alarmist
- Exponential growth underestimated (intuition trained on linear systems found it absurd)
- Supply chain fragility overlooked ("semiconductor shortages absurd")

**Result**: Catastrophic underpreparedness precisely because scenario seemed too extreme to take seriously

### 2008 Financial Crisis

**Dismissals Before**:
- "Housing prices always rise eventually" - 30-year crash seemed absurd based on historical pattern
- Sophisticated models suggested stability (models themselves embedded absurdity heuristic)
- Contagion mechanisms too abstract to seem real ("interconnectedness creating cascade absurd")

**Result**: Tail risks materialized that had been dismissed as absurdly overcomplicated

### Technology Adoption Errors

**Respected figures absurdly wrong** (applying heuristic):
- "Telephone will never replace telegraph" (seemed absurdly inefficient)
- "Personal computers unnecessary" (seemed absurdly expensive for individual use)
- "Internet never reaching mass adoption" (seemed absurdly complicated)

Each violated established patterns about efficiency/necessity, so seemed absurd.

## Overriding the Absurdity Heuristic: Practical Steps

### Step 1: Recognize When You're Using It (Metacognitive)
**What**: Notice when thinking "that's absurd/impossible/ridiculous"
**How**: Language signals - "absurd," "ridiculous," "unthinkable," "could never happen"
**Output**: Awareness you're deploying heuristic (not rigorous analysis)
**Example**: "Global pandemic shutting down economy? That's absurd" → STOP, this is heuristic talking

### Step 2: Distinguish Domains (5 min)
**What**: Apply different standards to different domains
**How**: Physics/math = heuristic most reliable; Complex systems = highly skeptical of dismissals
**Output**: Domain-calibrated skepticism
**Example**: Violating thermodynamics = actually absurd; Market crash = not absurd despite feeling absurd

### Step 3: Seek Base Rate Information (15-30 min)
**What**: Replace intuition with actual frequency data
**How**: Research historical occurrence rates, causal mechanisms
**Output**: Evidence-based probability vs intuitive dismissal
**Example**: Pandemics occurred 3-4 times per century historically → dismissing predictions unjustified

### Step 4: Consider Tail Scenarios Explicitly (20 min)
**What**: For high-impact possibilities, assign rough probabilities even if seeming "absurd"
**How**: Calculate expected value (Impact × Probability) even for low-P events
**Output**: Mathematical thinking vs intuitive dismissal
**Example**: 1% chance of catastrophe × catastrophic impact = worth serious preparation

### Step 5: Invert the Question (10 min)
**What**: Ask "why WOULDN'T this happen?" instead of "why would it?"
**How**: Identify specific mechanisms preventing it
**Output**: Often reveals reasons for dismissal weaker than thought
**Example**: "Why wouldn't financial crises recur?" → Nothing prevents them (inherent to credit systems)

### Step 6: Build Diverse Perspectives (Ongoing)
**What**: Consult people from different fields and track records
**How**: Domain experts who studied tail events, other cultures with different reference classes, science fiction writers
**Output**: Break consensus-group stickiness
**Example**: Epidemiologists didn't find pandemic absurd (domain expertise)

### Step 7: Implement Structured Pre-Mortems (30-60 min)
**What**: Before dismissing scenario, conduct pre-mortem
**How**: Assume absurd thing happened, what was causal chain? Are causes plausible? What early warnings?
**Output**: Testable prediction vs intuitive rejection
**Example**: "Assume pandemic happened. Supply chain disrupted, borders closed. Are these plausible? Yes."

## Balancing Dismissal with Openness

### Decision Framework

| Situation | Apply Heuristic? | Better Approach |
|-----------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Scientific claims violating known physics | Yes, but verify | Lean on heuristic, verify rigorously |
| Tail risks in complex systems | No | Probabilistic reasoning, scenario analysis |
| Resource allocation for rare high-impact | No | Expected value calculations |
| Time-constrained quick filtering | Yes | But flag for later review |
| Long-term strategy and planning | No | Explicitly model uncertainty and tails |
| Expert vs layperson predictions | Context-dependent | Evaluate track record, not plausibility |

### Calibration Principles

**Asymmetric Skepticism**: Apply high skepticism to absurdity dismissals when dealing with tail risks and complex systems

**Track Absurdity Predictions**: Keep record of things dismissed as absurd. Review regularly. Creates feedback calibrating heuristic use.

**Distinguish "I don't understand" from "It's impossible"**: Complex mechanisms seem absurd because beyond intuitive grasp - sign to study more, not dismiss.

**Respect Domain Expertise with Causal Reasoning**: Expert dismissal carries more weight than layperson's, but only if they've thought through mechanisms. "That's absurd" from expert isn't enough - ask WHY.

**Institutional Override**: Organizations can mandate scenario planning, stress testing, devil's advocacy to counteract individuals' absurdity dismissals.
