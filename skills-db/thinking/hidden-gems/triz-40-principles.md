# TRIZ 40 Inventive Principles: Genrich Altshuller's Systematic Framework for Innovation

**Source**: Genrich Altshuller - Soviet inventor, patent analyst
**Year**: 1946-1974 development
**Obscurity**: Tier 1 (⭐⭐⭐) - Very High outside engineering
**Domain**: 06-problem-solving
**Score**: 42/50

## Origin & Context

TRIZ (Theory of Inventive Problem Solving) emerged from Genrich Altshuller's analysis of over 200,000 patents starting in 1946. Working in the Soviet Navy's inventions-inspection department, Altshuller recognized that revolutionary inventions were actually solving problems previously solved in different industries. This sparked his lifetime commitment to systematizing innovation.

Despite being arrested in 1950 and sentenced to 25 years in the Vorkuta Gulag (released 1953 after Stalin's death), Altshuller continued developing TRIZ. His first paper appeared in 1956. He discovered that successful inventors employed a limited set of strategies - the 40 inventive principles - that transcended industry boundaries.

## Core Principles (The Foundation)

1. **Contradictions Drive Innovation**: Technical problems arise when improving one characteristic worsens another. Ideal solutions eliminate contradictions rather than accepting trade-offs.

2. **Patterns Are Universal**: The 40 principles represent proven strategies from patent history that apply across all technical domains.

3. **Systematic Over Random**: Innovation becomes routine and systematic rather than serendipitous through rigorous methodology.

4. **Ideality as North Star**: Systems evolve toward maximum beneficial effects with minimum costs/harm. Formula: Ideality = Benefits / (Costs + Harm).

5. **Evolution Follows Laws**: Technical systems evolve along predictable patterns - from macro to micro scale, mechanical to field-based, uniform to composite materials.

## The 40 Inventive Principles (Complete)

**Structural Segmentation (1-7)**:
1. **Segmentation** - Divide into independent parts, make sectional
2. **Extraction** - Separate interfering properties, extract necessary parts
3. **Local Quality** - Non-uniform structures, optimize each part for its function
4. **Asymmetry** - Transform symmetric to asymmetric for functional value
5. **Merging** - Combine identical/similar objects for efficiency
6. **Universality** - Objects perform multiple functions
7. **Nested Doll** - Place objects inside each other (Russian doll)

**Weight & Forces (8-10)**:
8. **Counter-weight** - Compensate weight with lifting force or aerodynamic/hydrodynamic forces
9. **Prior Counter-action** - Preload compensating forces
10. **Prior Action** - Perform required changes in advance

**Temporal & Spatial (11-17)**:
11. **Cushion in Advance** - Prepare emergency means beforehand
12. **Equi-potentiality** - Eliminate need to lift/lower objects
13. **Inversion** - Opposite actions, cooling vs heating, reversing sequences
14. **Spheroidality/Curvature** - Replace linear with curved, flat with spherical
15. **Dynamics** - Modify for optimal performance through variation
16. **Partial or Excessive Action** - Aim for more or less when optimal is difficult
17. **Transition to Another Dimension** - 1D→2D, 2D→3D transitions

**Oscillation & Fields (18-24)**:
18. **Mechanical Vibration** - Introduce oscillation
19. **Periodic Action** - Replace continuous with periodic impulses
20. **Continuity of Useful Action** - No breaks, full capacity operation
21. **Rushing Through** - High speed for harmful/hazardous operations
22. **Convert Harm into Benefit** - Use harmful factors positively
23. **Feedback** - Introduce or modify feedback systems
24. **Mediator** - Use intermediary objects

**Material Substitution (25-31)**:
25. **Self-service** - Objects serve themselves, auto-repair
26. **Copying** - Use inexpensive copies instead of expensive originals
27. **Inexpensive Short-Life** - Replace expensive permanent with cheap disposable
28. **Mechanical Substitution** - Replace with optical/thermal/chemical systems
29. **Pneumatics/Hydraulics** - Use gas/liquid instead of solid parts
30. **Flexible Membranes** - Use flexible shells, thin films
31. **Porous Materials** - Make porous or use pores for useful substances

**Property Changes (32-40)**:
32. **Optical Property Changes** - Change color, translucency, add luminescent traces
33. **Homogeneity** - Use same/similar materials for interacting objects
34. **Discarding and Recovery** - Reject after function or recover for reuse
35. **Parameter Changes** - Modify temperature, pressure, density
36. **Phase Transitions** - Use phenomena of phase change
37. **Thermal Expansion** - Use expansion/contraction, different coefficients
38. **Strong Oxidants** - Increase oxidation levels progressively
39. **Inert Atmosphere** - Replace environment with inert or vacuum
40. **Composite Materials** - Change from uniform to composite

## The Contradiction Matrix

- **39×39 grid** of engineering parameters
- Maps which principles historically resolved specific contradictions
- Parameters include: weight, length, speed, strength, temperature, energy, manufacturing precision, automation extent, productivity
- Each cell contains 0-4 principles that frequently resolved that contradiction
- Enables systematic access to proven solutions

## Step-by-Step Application Process

### Phase 1: Problem Definition (15 min)
- State specific technical challenge
- Identify functional requirements and constraints
- Clarify boundaries (system, subsystems, supersystem)
- Identify where/when problem occurs
- Define harmful and beneficial effects

### Phase 2: Identify Contradictions (20 min)
**Technical Contradiction**: Improving parameter A worsens parameter B
- Example: Stronger material increases weight

**Physical Contradiction**: Object must have opposite properties
- Example: Landing gear must be present (safety) and absent (drag)

### Phase 3: Access Knowledge Bases (10 min)
- For technical contradictions: Use contradiction matrix
- Map improving parameter (row) vs worsening parameter (column)
- Matrix suggests 0-4 relevant principles
- For physical contradictions: Use separation principles

### Phase 4: Generate Solutions (30-60 min)
- Translate abstract principles into specific solutions
- Develop multiple concepts per principle
- Combine technical knowledge with creative application
- Use substance-field analysis for complex problems

### Phase 5: Evaluate & Refine (30 min)
- Assess technical feasibility, resource requirements, costs
- Verify contradiction is actually eliminated (not shifted)
- Check for new contradictions introduced
- Align with laws of technical system evolution
- Prototype and test

## Real-World Applications

### Samsung Electronics (2003-2004)
**Problem**: Fast follower strategy hit limits, needed innovation
**TRIZ Application**: 
- Trained 1,000+ engineers (15 days formal + 7 days project-specific)
- Mandatory for career advancement
**Results**:
- 50 new patents in 2003
- Single DVD pickup project: $100M+ savings in 2004
- Transformation to innovation leader
- Super AMOLED displays, advanced smartphones

### Boeing Aircraft Design
**Problem**: Performance vs cost trade-offs in aircraft design
**TRIZ Application**: Identified contradictions previously accepted as inevitable
**Results**: Performance improvements + cost/complexity reductions simultaneously

### Paint Bottle Filling Machine
**Problem**: Automate rotating and sealing 10 lids without complex mechanics
**Technical Contradiction**: Automation (parameter 38) vs Device Complexity (parameter 36)
**Matrix Suggested**: Principles 15 (Dynamics), 24 (Mediator), 10 (Prior Action)
**Solution**: Cam mechanisms perform rotation + sealing through single component
**Result**: Simplified mechanics, full automation, production implementation
