# Commitment Devices - Behavioral Economics Tools

**Source**: Behavioral Economics (Thomas Schelling + modern applications)
**Year**: Schelling's foundational work + modern implementations
**Obscurity**: Tier 2 (⭐⭐) - High outside behavioral economics
**Domain**: 04-decision-making / 02-cognitive-biases
**Score**: 29/50

## Origin & Context

Commitment devices represent a sophisticated approach to managing the inherent conflict between long-term aspirations and short-term impulses. The theoretical foundation traces to economist Thomas Schelling's exploration of self-binding in strategic decision-making.

The classic metaphor comes from Homer's Odyssey: Odysseus had himself tied to the mast to resist the Sirens' song, acknowledging future weakness and taking preemptive action. This "Ulysses Pact" remains the archetypal commitment device - a conscious act of self-constraint made when willpower is strong to protect against future temptation.

## Core Principle

**Mechanism that restricts future choices to align with long-term goals**

A choice made in present to constrain future options, bridging gap between initial motivation and typical noncompliance over time.

## Core Principles

1. **Time-Inconsistent Preferences**: People exhibit hyperbolic discounting - value immediate rewards substantially more than future benefits

2. **Planning Self vs Doer Self**: Friday evening self wants to gym Monday; Monday morning self wants to stay in bed

3. **Precommitment When Strong**: Make binding decisions when willpower high to protect against future weakness

4. **External Constraint**: Outsource willpower from internal self to external environment/structure

5. **Cost of Deviation**: Create meaningful penalty for non-compliance (financial, social, psychological)

## Psychological Foundations

### Hyperbolic Discounting Problem

Individuals often value immediate rewards substantially more than future benefits, even when rational analysis suggests prioritizing long-term outcomes.

**Example**: Friday you genuinely want gym Monday. Monday arrives - bed becomes overwhelmingly attractive. The "planning self" has different preferences than "doer self."

### Key Mechanisms

**Commitment Escalation**: Having made initial investment, people invest further to justify prior commitment. Abandoning commitment = wasting initial investment (powerful incentive).

**Anticipated Regret**: Pre-committing creates fear of future remorse if failing to follow through. Not just financial loss, but shame of wasted money and abandoned commitment.

**Self-Control Enhancement**: Structurally harder to deviate from desired behavior - outsources willpower to environment instead of relying on momentary discipline.

## Types of Commitment Devices

### 1. Financial Commitment Devices

**Mechanism**: Financial stakes introduce potential loss if goals abandoned

**Study**: Ashraf & Karlan (2005) - Filipino banking "commitment account" where withdrawal abilities limited
**Result**: Participants saved 81% more than those with typical accounts

**Examples**:
- Pre-paid subscriptions (fitness classes, services)
- Deposits with forfeiture for non-compliance
- Automatic savings transfers creating friction to access funds
- Stakes on goal completion (StickK, Beeminder)

**Key**: Ethical graduated stakes for different risk tolerances (not one-size-fits-all penalty)

### 2. Social Commitment Devices

**Mechanism**: Leverage social pressure and accountability

**How**: Publicly declare goals, share progress with peers, join accountability groups

**Why It Works**: Fear of social disapproval if abandoning commitments

**Effectiveness**: Personality-dependent - those who thrive on external validation respond particularly well

**Examples**:
- Public goal declarations (social media, workplace)
- Accountability partner check-ins
- Community-based programs (Weight Watchers, AA)
- Peer support groups

### 3. Friction Commitment Devices

**Mechanism**: Add inconvenience to opting out of desired behaviors

**Examples**:
- Smaller lunch boxes (deter overeating)
- Scheduled tasks with automatic calendar blocking
- Website blockers during work hours
- Temptation bundling (enjoyable activities only during intended behaviors - podcast only while exercising)

**Key**: Creates psychological and structural barriers without formal penalties

### 4. Personal Strategy Devices

**Mechanism**: Self-imposed reminders and tracking

**Examples**:
- Habit-tracking apps
- Reminder systems
- Progress dashboards
- Scheduled commitments

**Limitation**: Less formally constraining than other types, relies more on self-discipline

## Real-World Examples

### Dan Ariely's CSA Pre-Payment

**Problem**: Wanted to increase fruit/vegetable consumption
**Device**: Pre-paid entire year of biweekly CSA deliveries
**Mechanism**: Sunk cost → accessing deliveries became easier option vs psychological burden of wasting prepaid funds
**Result**: Dramatically increased utilization

### Filipino Banking Commitment Accounts

**Study**: Ashraf & Karlan (2005)
**Device**: Restricted-withdrawal savings accounts
**Result**: 81% increase in savings vs standard accounts
**Significance**: Institutional commitment device provision by financial institutions

### Modern App Implementations

**Beeminder, StickK** (not detailed in sources but mentioned conceptually):
- Financial stakes on goal completion
- Automated tracking and enforcement
- Gradual stake escalation
- Social accountability integration

## Step-by-Step: Creating Effective Commitment Devices

### Step 1: Identify Time-Inconsistency Problem (10 min)
**What**: Diagnose where present preferences diverge from long-term goals
**How**: Honest self-assessment about behavioral patterns and trigger points where willpower fails
**Output**: Clear gap between "what you want to want" and "what you'll choose when tempted"
**Example**: Want to write daily (long-term) but choose Netflix when evening arrives (short-term)

### Step 2: Select Appropriate Mechanism Type (15 min)
**What**: Choose device type matching motivational structure
**How**: Consider what works for your personality and goal type
**Output**: Device category selection
**Example**: Financial if monetary incentives motivate; Social if peer accountability effective; Friction if reducing ease changes behavior

### Step 3: Establish Initial Investment (20 min)
**What**: Create upfront commitment triggering commitment escalation
**How**: Financial deposit, scheduled time blocks, public declaration
**Output**: Meaningful but not punitive initial stake
**Example**: $50 deposit forfeited if don't complete 30-day writing challenge

### Step 4: Build Accountability Mechanisms (30 min)
**What**: Incorporate monitoring adherence and reporting
**How**: Social devices = check-ins with partners; Financial = spending tracking; Personal = habit logging
**Output**: Regular accountability touchpoints
**Example**: Weekly check-in with accountability partner sharing progress

### Step 5: Create Graduated Consequences (Ongoing)
**What**: Allow partial success while penalizing systematic non-compliance
**How**: Not all-or-nothing but scaled penalties
**Output**: Resilient to occasional lapses
**Example**: Forgiveness for 1-2 misses per month, penalty after 3+

### Step 6: Review and Adjust (Monthly)
**What**: Assess effectiveness, modify as needed
**How**: Track completion rates, evaluate if device helping or creating resentment
**Output**: Refined commitment mechanism
**Example**: If too punitive → reduce stakes; if ineffective → increase accountability

## When Commitment Devices Work vs Backfire

### Conditions for Success

**Genuine Goal Alignment**: Must target authentically valued goals (not imposed against true preferences)

**Appropriate Time Horizons**: Clear temporal boundaries (6-month fitness commitment with monthly check-ins > vague indefinite targets)

**Proportionate Stakes**: Meaningful enough to motivate, not so severe as to trigger resentment/panic

**Supportive Social Context**: Others support goal or participate in similar commitments

### Backfiring Mechanisms

**Perceived Coercion**: Experienced as externally imposed → psychological reactance (motivation to reassert freedom by violating)

**Excessive Complexity**: Too intricate or time-consuming → friction in mechanism itself causes abandonment

**Values Misalignment**: Clashes with deeply held values → internal conflict vs resolution

**Moving Target Goals**: Unstable objectives → devices accumulate failure, demoralizing

**Financial Insufficiency**: Stakes too minimal fail to motivate; too high create genuine hardship

## Relationship to Schelling Fences

Commitment devices often work WITH Schelling Fences (salient focal points) rather than against them.

**Connection**: Schelling's work on focal points and self-binding suggests certain boundaries become psychologically salient - natural locations for commitment.

**Examples**:
- January 1st (New Year's resolutions)
- Specific financial thresholds
- Visible markers serving as boundaries

Commitment devices use these socially recognized milestones as natural demarcation lines for commitment periods.
