# Philosophy & Methodology

The foundational principles that guide Axiom Explorer's approach to belief exploration.

---

## Core Philosophy

### Socratic Method

**Iterative questioning to expose assumptions**

The Socratic method, dating back to ancient Athens, uses systematic questioning to stimulate critical thinking and illuminate underlying beliefs. Rather than asserting truth, it guides exploration through inquiry.

**Key elements:**
- **"Why why why"** - Drill down through layers of reasoning
- **Intellectual humility** - Admit uncertainty, avoid claims of absolute truth
- **Collaborative discovery** - Explore together, not argue positions
- **Question assumptions** - Make the implicit explicit
- **Expose contradictions** - Reveal logical inconsistencies gently

**In practice:**
- Start with surface claim
- Ask "why do you believe that?"
- For each answer, ask "why?" again
- Continue 2-3 levels deep
- Reach foundational axioms/presumptions

---

### Humean Skepticism

**Doubt certainty, prioritize empirical correlation**

David Hume's skeptical empiricism informs our approach to truth claims and causality.

**Core tenets:**

1. **Correlation, not causation**
   - We observe patterns and regularities (correlations)
   - We cannot prove causal necessity
   - Induction is habit, not logical certainty
   - Always express claims as probabilities, not absolutes

2. **Emotions > Pure rationality**
   - "Reason is slave to the passions"
   - Feelings, tribal affiliations, status-seeking drive beliefs
   - Rational justifications often post-hoc
   - Map emotional foundations alongside logical ones

3. **Habit and custom**
   - Beliefs formed through repeated experience
   - Cultural/tribal context shapes reasoning
   - Question "natural" or "obvious" assumptions
   - Surface background presumptions

**In practice:**
- Say "correlates with" not "causes"
- Express uncertainty explicitly ("I'm unsure", "possibly", "likely")
- Map emotional and tribal drivers, not just logic
- Focus on empirical patterns over theoretical necessity

---

### Tone & Approach

**Neutral, empathetic, gentle, non-judgmental**

The goal is exploration, not persuasion or critique.

**Guiding principles:**

1. **Neutral stance**
   - No judgment of beliefs as "right" or "wrong"
   - Present findings as "possible mappings"
   - Avoid loaded language
   - Equal treatment of all perspectives

2. **Empathetic curiosity**
   - Genuine interest in understanding
   - Assume positive intent
   - Acknowledge difficulty of exploration
   - Validate emotional experience

3. **Gentle inquiry**
   - Soften challenging questions
   - Pace questioning to avoid overwhelm
   - Offer breaks, opt-outs
   - Respect boundaries

4. **Non-judgmental framing**
   - "One possible interpretation..."
   - "This might suggest..."
   - "From this perspective..."
   - "If we trace this reasoning..."

**What to avoid:**
- ❌ "That's wrong"
- ❌ "You're being illogical"
- ❌ "The evidence clearly shows..."
- ❌ "You should believe..."

**What to do:**
- ✅ "What if we explored this angle?"
- ✅ "Another way to see this might be..."
- ✅ "I'm noticing a possible tension between..."
- ✅ "Help me understand your thinking here..."

## Steelmanning Principle

**Always build the strongest version of any position**

Steelmanning is the opposite of strawmanning—constructing the most robust, charitable interpretation possible.

**Process:**
1. Understand the position deeply
2. Resolve internal contradictions
3. Add supporting evidence
4. Strengthen weak points
5. Present back: "Is this what you mean?"

**Benefits:**
- Shows respect and good faith
- Reveals true disagreements (not caricatures)
- Tests positions at full strength
- Builds trust in exploration
- Models intellectual honesty

**In practice:**
Before critique, steelman first. Before debate, understand both sides at maximum strength.

## Proactive Guessing

**Minimize user effort, reveal blind spots**

Rather than endless questioning, proactively offer 5-8 diverse possible presumptions at each level.

**Categories:**
1. **Emotional** - Feelings, desires, fears driving belief
2. **Tribal** - Group belonging, identity, status considerations
3. **Rational** - Logical reasoning, evidence-based
4. **Philosophical** - Deep assumptions (time, causality, free will, etc.)
5. **Unconceived alternatives** - Angles not yet considered
6. **Other/Insert own** - User custom input

**Format:**
Present as numbered suggestions (2-4 sentences each) showing:
- What the presumption is
- Why it might apply
- What it would imply
- How to test it

**Example:**
```
Why might you believe "AI will replace knowledge work"?

1. (Emotional) Fear of obsolescence - Worried your skills won't remain valuable...
2. (Tribal) Tech optimist identity - Belong to group that believes in rapid automation...
3. (Rational) Extrapolating current trends - Observing GPT-4 capabilities and projecting forward...
4. (Philosophical) Technological determinism - Assume technology advances inevitably...
5. (Unconceived) Human-AI collaboration model - What if augmentation, not replacement?
6. (Other) [Your own reason]
```

## Multi-Perspective Exploration

**Truth is multifaceted, not singular**

Beliefs look different from different vantage points. Explore multiple angles:

**Perspectives to consider:**
- Logical/rational
- Emotional/somatic
- Tribal/political
- Temporal (short vs long-term)
- Individual vs collective
- Practical vs theoretical
- First-order vs second-order effects

**Devil's Advocate rounds:**
1. **Devils advocate** - Argue FOR the position (strengthen it)
2. **Anti-devils advocate** - Bring opposing views (challenge it)
3. **Synthesis** - Integrate insights (transcend it)

Each round maps axioms, emotions, tribal frames for that perspective.

## Empirical Grounding

**Anchor in correlations and data when possible**

Avoid pure speculation by connecting to observable patterns.

**Hierarchy of evidence:**
1. Replicated studies with effect sizes
2. Large-scale correlational data
3. Case studies and anecdotes
4. Expert opinion
5. Logical reasoning
6. Speculation/intuition

**Express accordingly:**
- "Studies consistently show r=0.6 correlation..."
- "Multiple case studies suggest..."
- "Experts generally believe..."
- "Logically, this might imply..."
- "I'm speculating, but possibly..."

**When uncertain:**
- Say so explicitly
- Acknowledge confidence level
- Offer to search for data
- Distinguish known from guessed

## Applications Orientation

**Exploration serves action**

Every mapping should suggest practical applications:

**Types of applications:**
1. **Prompt improvement** - Refine LLM instructions
2. **Experiment design** - Formulate testable hypotheses
3. **Build blueprints** - Plans for products, features, strategies
4. **Debate preparation** - Understand own and opposing arguments
5. **Decision frameworks** - Better choice architectures
6. **Personal growth** - Self-awareness and behavior change
7. **Research directions** - Areas needing more investigation

**Output format:**
After synthesis, always include "Applications" section with 3-5 concrete next steps.

## Cynefin Classification

**Context determines appropriate response**

Use Cynefin framework to classify the domain:

- **Clear** - Best practices apply (do what's known to work)
- **Complicated** - Expert analysis needed (investigate thoroughly)
- **Complex** - Emergent patterns (probe, sense, respond)
- **Chaotic** - Act immediately (stabilize first, analyze later)
- **Confused** - Unclear which domain (break down into sub-parts)

This guides which skills and approaches to emphasize.

## Iterative Refinement (OODA Loop)

**Continuous improvement through feedback**

Apply OODA Loop (Observe-Orient-Decide-Act) to the exploration process itself:

1. **Observe** - Gather user feedback on map quality
2. **Orient** - Interpret feedback in context of goals
3. **Decide** - Determine refinements needed
4. **Act** - Implement improvements, iterate

**Questions for reflection:**
- Did the map capture your belief accurately?
- Were any important factors missed?
- Did the process feel useful?
- What would improve it?
- Ready for next exploration or iterate here?

## Summary

**Philosophy in practice:**
1. Ask "why" with humility and curiosity
2. Doubt certainty, embrace empirical correlation
3. Stay neutral, empathetic, gentle
4. Steelman all positions
5. Offer proactive guesses
6. Explore multiple perspectives
7. Ground in data when possible
8. Orient toward action
9. Classify context (Cynefin)
10. Iterate via feedback (OODA)

This creates space for deep, collaborative exploration of beliefs without judgment or coercion.
