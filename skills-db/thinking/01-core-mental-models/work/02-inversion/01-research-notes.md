# Research Notes: Inversion

## Origin
- **Author**: Charlie Munger (popularized), Carl Gustav Jacob Jacobi (original mathematical principle)
- **Year**: Mathematical principle from 1800s, applied to business/life by Munger 1990s-2000s
- **Source**: "Invert, always invert" - Jacobi's maxim; Munger speeches/writings; Shane Parrish (Farnam Street/Great Mental Models Vol 1)
- **Key Quote**: "All I want to know is where I'm going to die so I'll never go there" - Charlie Munger

## Core Concept
Inversion flips conventional problem-solving by asking "What would cause failure?" instead of "How do I succeed?". This reveals critical failure points that forward thinking obscures. Rather than optimizing for progress, you identify and systematically avoid what leads to disaster.

## Key Examples from Research
1. **WWII Meteorology**: Munger inverted "predict weather" → "when should flights be grounded?" - prevented pilot deaths
2. **Physical Problem**: Head stuck in trap - inverted from "pull head through" → "push body through" - instant solution
3. **Business Planning**: Instead of "how to succeed?" → "what guarantees failure?" - avoid those paths systematically
4. **Personal Life**: "Where will I die?" → systematically avoid those paths

## Process Steps (Extracted)
1. Reframe goal as negative: "What would cause failure?"
2. List specific failure conditions
3. Convert failures into avoidance principles
4. Apply two-track analysis (rational + psychological)

## When to Use
- Stuck on forward-thinking solution
- High-stakes decisions requiring error avoidance
- Multiple psychological biases could cloud judgment
- Long-term success depends on consistency over brilliance
- Need to identify critical failure modes

## Anti-Patterns
- Don't just list fears without converting to action
- Don't ignore psychological biases (two-track analysis required)
- Don't focus only on inversion (combine with forward thinking)
- Inversion reveals what to avoid, not necessarily what to do
